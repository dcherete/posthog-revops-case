What I inherited

When I joined Brasil Paralelo's CRM team, the company had a database of over 6 million leads and 800 thousand active subscribers. Decisions about who to contact, when, and through which channel were based on intuition and list segmentation: recency, product category, campaign history. There was no score. No model. No way to know which segments were worth the channel cost and which were destroying value.

The first model I built was a resubscription propensity model for ex-members: churned subscribers with the highest probability of reactivating. The second was a lead score for non-members: people who had never subscribed but were statistically likely to convert with the right outreach.

Both models were validated against real revenue outcomes before being deployed in production. This is not the standard practice. Most scores are deployed on the assumption that a higher score means a better lead. The validation work showed how wrong that assumption can be, and how expensive it is to act on an unvalidated score at scale.

What this means in practice Without score validation, band D would have been deployed alongside band A. The model correctly identified that band D destroys value: every person reached in that band costs more than the revenue they generate. Deploying to bands A and B only — the validated decision — concentrates spend on the segment where the model is predictive and the economics are positive.

Breakeven calibration Finding the deployment threshold

Once score bands are validated, the next question is where to draw the line. The breakeven threshold is the minimum score at which expected revenue covers the cost of reaching that person.

The formula is straightforward. The hard part is knowing your true cost per person and your expected revenue per conversion — both require real data from prior campaigns, not assumptions.

breakeven score = cost per person / (avg ticket × delivery rate × conversion rate at threshold)

For the EXM v6.6 model, the cost per person was derived from platform and ops data from prior campaigns. Two thresholds were calculated:

≥ 0.030
Conservative deployment
15,000 people in scope. Expected conversion: 6.3%. Validated as profitable at D1. Used for production deployment.
≥ 0.018
Economic breakeven
31,500 people in scope. Expected conversion: 4.2%. Profitable at D7 projection. Used to define maximum addressable pool.
Why two thresholds matter The conservative threshold tells you where to deploy today. The economic breakeven tells you the size of the total addressable pool that the model can profitably serve — useful for capacity planning and for knowing when the model is getting close to exhausting its high-quality segment.

Two different scoring contexts What transfers and what does not

The methodology transfers directly. The data sources, outcome definition, and feedback loop are different. Being precise about these differences matters for designing the PostHog experiment correctly.

Brasil Paralelo — B2C subscription
Score inputs
Behavioral (CRM engagement, site sessions, content consumption), transactional (failed payments, churn date), demographic (income, region)
Outcome definition
Resubscription within 7 days of first contact. Binary. Clear.
Feedback loop
7 days to D1 result, 30 days to full signal. Fast iteration.
Cost of a wrong threshold
Wasted channel spend at scale. Recoverable within one campaign cycle.
PostHog — B2B PLG
Score inputs
Firmographic only: company size, industry, estimated revenue. No behavioral signals from product usage.
Outcome definition
ARR invoiced at 6 and 12 months. Continuous, not binary. Must be defined before the experiment runs.
Feedback loop
6 to 12 months to meaningful revenue signal. Slower iteration, higher stakes per decision.
Cost of a wrong threshold
TAM time misallocated for 12+ months on accounts that would have grown self-serve anyway. Hard to recover.

Questions before solutions

Três perguntas determinam o instrumento certo.

How does PostHog define incremental ARR?
An account growing from $8K to $30K with a TAM involved may have arrived there with or without the rep. Without a committed definition of what counts as TAM contribution versus organic product growth, any analysis of the ICP score is reinterpretable after the result appears.
What is the historical volume in Salesforce?
The volume of accounts with 12 months of observed ARR determines the method. With few cases, ML is not the right instrument.
Does Vitally have point-in-time behavioral data?
For a retrospective analysis to be valid, signals like products activated and MRR trajectory would need to be available as they were at the moment of first rep touch, not as they are today.
This deliverable shows what I built. What PostHog needs to resolve the ICP problem depends on answers that only exist on the inside.

Where prediction does transfer Usage trajectory

The ICP score problem is hard to validate because the outcome is slow and entangled with the product. There is a different prediction problem at PostHog where none of those constraints apply.

PostHog is usage-based. Every product has a measurable limit: events ingested, session recordings, feature flag evaluations. Usage per account is observable week by week, directly from the billing system.

The question is well-defined: given an account's current usage trajectory, when will they hit their plan limit? Accounts tracking to breach within 45 to 60 days are the signal. The TAM reaches out not with a generic upsell, but with a specific observation.

"At your current growth rate, you will exceed your plan in August. Here is what we would recommend."

This transfers from the BP methodology because the three problems that made ICP validation hard do not apply here. The outcome is observable in weeks. There is no attribution question between rep and product. The data already exists in the billing system.

What the model would need
Weekly usage per product
Events, recordings, flags — per account, per product, per week. Already tracked in PostHog's billing system.
Growth rate and trend
Week-over-week change. Is usage accelerating, stable, or declining? Acceleration compresses time-to-limit faster than a flat trend.
Plan limit per product
The ceiling for each account's current plan. Subtracting current usage gives headroom; dividing headroom by weekly growth gives weeks-to-limit.
TAM output
A ranked list of accounts by days-to-limit, with current plan, usage trajectory, and recommended next plan. Refreshed weekly. The conversation writes itself.
