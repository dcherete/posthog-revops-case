# Compensation Operations: Before and After

This document maps the compensation operations problems I solved at Brasil Paralelo against the analogous problems PostHog has today. It is not a claim that the contexts are identical — B2C subscription and B2B PLG are different motions. It is a demonstration that the operational thinking transfers.

---

## The parallel

| | Brasil Paralelo (what I inherited) | PostHog (what exists today) |
|---|---|---|
| Team size | 22 sales reps | Unknown externally — estimated 8–15 TAEs and TAMs based on headcount and ARR |
| Compensation model | Tiered multipliers on revenue: 80% attainment = 2.5% of revenue, 100% = 5%, 130% = 7.5% | Linear uncapped sliding scale on ARR growth, with cross-sell multiplier per product count |
| How commission was calculated | Manually, in spreadsheets. ~4 hours per month to apure, reconcile, and generate payouts | Manually, by one person (Ops & Finance). No documented model, no automation |
| Attainment visibility | Reps found out at month close | Reps find out when the quarterly commission email arrives |
| Quota basis | Revenue generated by each rep in the month | ARR invoiced (TAE) or ARR growth in book (TAM) |
| Ramp handling | Ramping reps had adjusted targets during onboarding period | First 3 months: 100% OTE fixed per handbook |
| SPIFs and conditional accelerators | Leadership proposed product-specific accelerators ("this product counts as 2 sales") when adoption needed a push | Cross-sell multiplier (0.7x to 1.5x+) creates a structural incentive, but no mechanism for tactical product-specific pushes |
| Model evolution | Moved from tiered multipliers to OTE structure — required projecting historical data under the new model to justify the change | No documented evolution path — the current model may need adjustment as the team scales and PLG attribution becomes more complex |

## Problem 1 — Manual apuration at scale

**Brasil Paralelo:** When I inherited the system, the monthly commission apuration took approximately 4 hours. This involved pulling revenue data, matching it to each rep's targets, applying the correct multiplier tier, reconciling exceptions (refunds, disputed deals, account transfers), and generating the payout breakdown for each of 22 reps.

As the team grew to 40 reps, the manual process would have become unmanageable. I rebuilt the system in BigQuery: staging tables for rep targets and tier thresholds, fact tables for monthly revenue by rep, and a transformation layer that applied the multiplier logic and output the attainment and payout per rep. The apuration time dropped from 4 hours to 1 hour — and the 1 hour was spent reviewing exceptions and corrections, not running calculations.

**PostHog equivalent:** Fraser currently runs the entire compensation process manually. The handbook documents the mechanics (OTE split, sliding scale, cross-sell multiplier, clawback rules) but there is no documented automation. As the team grows from its current size toward the scale implied by the $100M ARR target, manual apuration will become the bottleneck.

**What I would build in the first 60 days:** a Salesforce-connected attainment model that calculates quarterly commission per rep automatically, flags exceptions for human review, and generates the payout breakdown without manual calculation. The output is the same quarterly commission email Fraser sends today — but produced in minutes, not hours, and with full audit trail.

## Problem 2 — No attainment visibility during the period

**Brasil Paralelo:** Reps found out how they performed at month close. There was no mid-month signal. This created two problems: reps could not adjust behavior in time to change outcomes, and leadership had no early warning when a rep was tracking to miss.

I did not solve this fully — the system I built was still a monthly apuration engine. But the data infrastructure I created made it possible to run the calculation at any point during the month, not just at close. What was missing was the cadence and the dashboard to surface it.

**PostHog equivalent:** The same problem exists, compounded by the fact that the compensation cycle is quarterly rather than monthly. A TAE or TAM currently has no real-time view of their attainment, pipeline coverage relative to quota, or projected end-of-quarter payout. By the time the quarterly commission email arrives, there is nothing to do with the information.

**What I would build:** a weekly attainment snapshot for each rep showing current attainment percentage, open pipeline value, estimated close rate based on stage history, and projected end-of-quarter payout at current trajectory. This is a Salesforce report, not a complex model. The data already exists — it just is not being surfaced.

## Problem 3 — Quota setting without a documented model

**Brasil Paralelo:** The quota was set as 30% above the revenue projection derived from rep capacity data — conversion rate, revenue per lead, average lead time, and the 7-3-1 activity metric (7 approaches, 3 responses, 1 sale). I built and maintained that capacity model. The 30% stretch target was a leadership decision, but the underlying projection was mine.

When the team grew, we extended the model to account for ramping reps — their targets were adjusted during the onboarding period to reflect lower expected output. When we moved to OTE, the same capacity model was used to project what historical performance would have looked like under the new structure, giving leadership the data to make the decision.

**PostHog equivalent:** the handbook documents how commission is paid once quota is set, but not how quota is determined. The relationship between OTE, deal size, win rate, and the resulting quota is not documented. This means quota is currently a judgment call — which works at small scale but becomes increasingly hard to defend as the team grows and reps start asking why their number is what it is.

**What I would build:** a quota setting model with three inputs — the top-down ARR target for the sales-led motion, the bottom-up capacity estimate per rep based on average deal size and win rate, and the ramp adjustment for reps in their first quarter. The output is a quota per rep that is both ambitious and defensible. When leadership wants to change the model (as Brasil Paralelo's leadership did when they moved to OTE), the historical projection is already there.

## Problem 4 — Justifying a model change

**Brasil Paralelo:** When the commercial director wanted to move from tiered multipliers to OTE, the question was whether the new model would produce better or worse outcomes for reps and for the company. I ran the historical commission data through the OTE model and generated the comparison. The projection showed which reps would have earned more, which would have earned less, and what the total compensation cost would have been under each model in each of the prior 12 months.

That projection is what convinced leadership to make the change. Not an argument, not an opinion — a model.

**PostHog equivalent:** the current linear uncapped model is well-designed for the current stage. But as the team scales and the PLG attribution problem becomes more acute, there will be pressure to adjust — to add accelerators for specific products, to differentiate quota by account type, or to build in a floor that protects the company in low-attainment quarters. When that conversation happens, the RevOps function needs to be able to project what the proposed change would have produced historically before it goes into production.

**What I would build:** a scenario model that takes any proposed change to the compensation structure and projects its impact on historical attainment and payout data. This is the same thing I built at Brasil Paralelo, adapted for the PostHog data model.

## What is different about the PostHog context

Honesty about the gaps matters more than a clean narrative.

**B2B deal dynamics:** At Brasil Paralelo, revenue was subscription-based and arrived in predictable monthly increments. At PostHog, TAEs manage individual deals with cycles of weeks and deal sizes of $20K+. The attainment calculation is the same in principle but the data structure is different — deal stages, close probabilities, and the distinction between invoiced ARR and contracted ARR create complexity that did not exist in the subscription model I operated.

**PLG attribution:** The hardest problem in the PostHog compensation model has no equivalent at Brasil Paralelo. When a TAE closes a contract with an account that was already generating $800/month via self-serve, how much of that revenue is the rep's contribution? I have never had to solve this problem in production. I have a framework for how I would approach it — quasi-experimental design, holdout groups, attribution taxonomy in Salesforce — but I have not operated it.

**Salesforce as the system of record:** Brasil Paralelo's compensation system was built on BigQuery with Looker Studio for reporting. PostHog uses Salesforce and Vitally. The logic transfers, the tools do not. I would need ramp time on Salesforce administration that I do not currently have.

---

## The honest summary

I did not design the compensation model at Brasil Paralelo. I inherited it, operationalized it, automated it, and when leadership wanted to change it I generated the evidence that made the decision possible. I grew with the system from 22 to 40 reps and built the infrastructure that would allow it to scale further.

PostHog needs someone to do the same thing — take a compensation model that is well-designed but manually operated, build the infrastructure to run it at scale, and be the person who can model the implications of any change before it ships. That is work I have done. The context is different. The operational thinking is the same.
