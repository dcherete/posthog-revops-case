What I inherited

When I joined Brasil Paralelo, the commercial team had 22 reps and no reporting infrastructure. Revenue performance was measured after the fact, at month close, by pulling data from three disconnected systems and assembling it manually. The question of which rep closed which deal was resolved in a spreadsheet. Reps found out how they had performed when the apuration email arrived.

The monthly growth review did not exist as a system. It existed as a recurring scramble that produced a number too late to act on.

What you see below is what I built over approximately two years: a BigQuery attribution model connecting five data sources, a pipeline stage framework with SLA tracking, 14 operational metrics refreshed every 30 minutes, and a weekly cadence that turned all of it into a decision loop the commercial lead could actually run.

Sales CRM

Every deal with its stage history, assigned rep, lead source, and close date. A deal could be created by one rep and closed by another; the attribution model resolved this via QUALIFY ROW_NUMBER() prioritizing exact email match before falling back to temporal proximity.

Chat platform

Conversation history between reps and prospects, joined to CRM deals via prospect ID. A 2-hour lag was subtracted from card creation timestamps to handle Zapier processing delays; without this correction, early messages fell outside the join window.

Attribution model: the core

Resolved four transaction types in a single pass: tracked (rep captured at point of sale), untracked (no rep, assigned via lookup rules), canceled assignment (original rep removed and reassigned), transferred (deal moved between reps). Priority: transferred first, then canceled, then untracked, then original.

Transaction system

Revenue data from the billing system including subscription cycles and payment retries. A cd_deal_of_cycle field deduplicated retry charges; without this, a subscription with 3 payment attempts in one month appeared as 3 separate deals in the conversion rate denominator.

Dashboard layer

14 report sections built on top of the attribution model, refreshed every 30 minutes. Each section answered a specific operational question for the commercial lead. The weekly review pulled from this system so every conversation was grounded in the same data everyone could verify.

Compensation model

The final output of the pipeline. Attainment per rep per week, multiplier application, and commission calculation fed directly into the monthly apuration. The model automated what previously took 4 hours of manual work down to 1 hour of corrections review.

Pipeline stages and SLA tracking Click each stage

Every deal progressed through five stages. We tracked average time in each stage and defined SLAs. Reps were accountable for moving the pipeline within defined windows. Deals past the SLA threshold without stage movement were flagged in the weekly planning meeting.

List
CRM-selected leads for active outbound prospecting. Criteria: behavioral, transactional, and socioeconomic signals.
Selected
Inbound leads: site chat, failed transactions (denied, abandoned, expired), and approved transactions flagged for upsell.
List: outbound origin

Leads from the CRM database based on behavioral signals (content engagement, platform activity), transactional signals (purchase history, churn date), and socioeconomic profile. These had a prior brand relationship. The rep was responsible for first contact.

Selected: inbound origin

Higher intent than List leads. Chat leads initiated contact via the website. Failed transactions had already tried to buy. Upsell-flagged accounts were existing customers identified for cross-sell or upgrade. These typically converted faster and at higher ticket.

Won
Transaction confirmed. Rep receives attribution credit. Moves to compensation apuration.
Lost
Did not convert. Lost reason recorded. May re-enter pipeline in a future CRM cycle.
Open
In progress. Flagged if past SLA threshold without stage movement.
Note on the commercial role Marketing generated direct sales independently. The commercial team's primary function was upsell, expanding ticket value on top of what marketing already sold. This makes the commercial motion more similar to PostHog's TAM motion (expansion from existing customers) than to a traditional new business sales team.

Two different businesses, two different problems Why the context matters

The system above solved a specific problem in a specific context. Before mapping it to PostHog, it is worth being precise about what is structurally different, because the hardest problem in the PostHog comp model does not exist in the previous context at all.

Previous role — B2C subscription
TEAM SIZE
22 to 40 reps. High volume, low ticket, daily closes.
COMMERCIAL ROLE
Upsell and ticket expansion on top of what marketing sold. No product-led motion in parallel.
ATTRIBUTION QUESTION
Which rep closed which deal when multiple reps touched the same lead?
COMPENSATION QUESTION
Did this rep generate this revenue? Answer: almost always yes. No product doing it in parallel.
PostHog — B2B PLG
TEAM SIZE
Estimated 8 to 15 TAEs and TAMs. Low volume, high ticket ($20K+ ARR min), quarterly cycles.
COMMERCIAL ROLE
TAMs expand accounts already paying via self-serve. The product closes roughly 50% of revenue with no rep involvement at all.
ATTRIBUTION QUESTION
Was it the rep or the product that drove this revenue? This question has no clean answer today.
COMPENSATION QUESTION
How much of the ARR a TAM closed would have arrived organically anyway? The current comp model does not separate these, and the job description explicitly names this as unsolved.
